It’s a Wildfire!

Wildfires, also known as uncontrolled flames that occur in wildland vegetation, have a long history of destroying ecosystems such as forests, grasslands, and savannas. However, wildfires are not limited to specific geographies or ecosystems, and they have occurred naturally for millions of years (National Geographic Society, 2022).

They are a natural feature of many ecosystems and can have beneficial effects on the environment, such as supporting plant community regeneration and nutrient cycling. However, wildfires can cause major damage to property, infrastructure, and natural resources, as well as pose risk to human health and safety when they occur in inhabited regions or other sensitive situations.

Wildfires move quickly because they spread rapidly and produce high temperatures that can reach several thousand degrees Celsius. Moreover, it emits enormous volumes of smoke and ash, which can degrade air quality and visibility, as well as represent a health risk to humans and animals. Depending on the fuel conditions and response efforts, wildfires can burn for days, weeks, or even months.

These fires are caused by a number of natural and man-made factors. Lightning strikes, human activity, climate and meteorological conditions, fuel conditions, and natural events are some of the most common causes of wildfires. It's worth noting that many wildfires are started by a combination of these variables. Human activity, for example, may contribute to the initiation of a fire, but dry vegetation and strong winds can cause the fire to spread swiftly.

Types of Wildfire
The Northwest Fire Science Consortium (n.d.) defines three categories of forest fires: ground, surface, and crown. During a wildfire, it is fairly uncommon to see all three types of fire at the same time. The amount of each type, however, can vary dramatically from day to day, or even minute to minute, depending on factors such as fuel availability, topography, and weather conditions. The interaction of fuel, geography, and weather influences fire behavior, and any changes in these parameters can result in a ground fire shifting into a surface fire, a surface fire escalating into a crown fire, or vice versa.
Type of Wildfire
Ground
Mostly devour loose objects near or on the ground's surface, such as lichen, moss, needles, shrubs, herbaceous vegetation, tiny trees, and saplings. These fires spread through fiery combustion and play an important part in the spread of a fire. They have the potential to become more intense, resulting in blistering or probable consumption of the forest canopy, similar to the features of crown fires. Surface fire behavior is affected by a variety of factors, including the abundance of surface fuel, low fuel moisture content, steep slopes, high wind speeds, long surface flame lengths, proximity of tree crowns to the ground, and the density and arrangement of tree crowns (Northwest Fire Science Consortium, n.d., pp.1-2).

Type of Wildfire
Crown
Are often started by surface fires and devour forest canopy fuels such live and dead foliage/branches, tree lichens, and tall shrubs located well above the surface fuels. Crown fires can be classified as passive or aggressive. Passive crown fires, sometimes known as torching, involve the burning of individual trees or small groups of trees. Active crown fires, also known as running crown fires, on the other hand, produce a continuous wall of flames that extends from the surface to the canopy fuel layers, as shown in the accompanying shot. Active crown fires travel over the canopy from one tree crown to another (Northwest Fire Science Consortium, n.d., p.2).

ENVIRONMENTAL, SOCIAL AND ECONOMIC IMPACTS OF WILDFIRE

Wildfires can have serious consequences for the environment, society, and economy. These consequences can differ depending on the severity and duration of the fire, as well as the location and values under risk.

Wildfires can have both immediate and long-term environmental consequences. Wildfires can cause immediate damage to ecosystems, including habitat loss and biodiversity loss, in the short term. Wildfires can change the structure and composition of ecosystems over time, making them more vulnerable to future flames.

Furthermore, wildfires can have serious social consequences. They can cause individuals to be displaced from their homes and pose health hazards from smoke and ash. Wildfires can also wreak havoc on transportation and communication networks, making it difficult for people to get critical materials and information.

The economic repercussions of wildfires, on the other hand, include the destruction of homes and other infrastructure, the loss of money from tourism and other businesses, and the costs of fire suppression and rehabilitation. In rare circumstances, wildfires can have long-term economic consequences, such as decreased property prices and a reduction in local economies.
